# Character tactics

Who is in the story — including the storyteller — and how to position each role.

## Cut to the Chase {#cut-to-the-chase}

**Essence.** When your audience is drifting, abandon your build-up and jump straight to the moment of action, emotion, or insight that matters most.

**When to reach for it.** Whenever you can feel the room slipping away mid-story (in a meeting, a pitch, or a presentation) and a slow recovery is no longer an option.

**Why it works.** Boredom is the single most reliable killer of stories. The cure is not to talk faster but to skip ahead to the part the audience would have wanted to hear if you had started there. By cutting to the action, the emotion, or the lesson, you reset attention and force yourself to put the load-bearing moment up front instead of buried in setup.

**How to apply.**
- Jump to the pivotal moment of action or crisis and start describing what happened.
- Jump to the moment of peak emotion (shock, relief, dismay, joy) and let the feeling carry the listener back into the story.
- Jump to the lesson learned and then rewind only as far as needed to show how you got there.
- If even that is not landing, stop talking and turn the moment into a question that pulls the audience in.

## Drive Stories {#drive-stories}

**Essence.** Build a motivating team culture by collecting real stories of when people felt autonomous, skilled, and connected to purpose, plus the times they did not.

**When to reach for it.** When you need to inspire a team through change, hard work, or uncertain reward, and slogans about mission are not going to do the job on their own.

**Why it works.** Money and instructions are weak fuel for sustained effort; people stay motivated when they feel in control of their work, are getting better at it, and can see how it serves something larger. Stories that name specific moments of feeling those drives (or being deprived of them) are far more vivid than abstract talk about culture. Negative examples are as useful as positive ones because they show what to protect against.

**How to apply.**
- Surface autonomy stories: moments when people felt in charge of how, where, and with whom they worked, and the opposite moments when control was missing.
- Surface mastery stories: moments when skills clearly improved or fell short of what the job demanded.
- Surface purpose stories: moments when work clearly served a greater good, and moments when the bigger picture went missing.
- Develop these raw moments into teachable team stories about how we work, how we grow, and why we do it.

## Hero & Guide {#hero-and-guide}

**Essence.** Cast the audience as the hero of the story and yourself as the experienced guide who helps them get where they want to go.

**When to reach for it.** Any time you are tempted to make yourself the centre of attention in a pitch, talk, or piece of marketing, and especially when the audience needs to see themselves succeeding to believe your offer.

**Why it works.** People care about their own story, not yours. When you make them the hero, they imagine acting on what you say; when you make yourself the hero, they tune out. The guide is still essential: a hero without a wise mentor rarely makes it through the journey. So you keep your authority by offering perspective, a gift, and a path, while letting the audience carry the action.

**How to apply.**
- Map where your audience is today, what their problem is, and where they want to be.
- Decide what kind of guide you are for them: explorer, sage, rebel, defender, muse, or warrior.
- Name the specific gift you bring (knowledge, tools, courage, safety, creativity, or focus).
- Tell the story of their journey, not yours, with you cast in a supporting mentor role.
- Borrow a familiar story shape, like a rise from nothing, recovery from a slump, or a hard road with no shortcut, to give the journey a recognisable arc.

## Trust Me, I'm an Expert {#trust-me-im-an-expert}

**Essence.** Earn the audience's trust by telling a story in which your values and judgement showed up in action, not by reciting your credentials.

**When to reach for it.** When you need to be believed on something important and listing facts or titles alone will not close the credibility gap with your audience.

**Why it works.** Expertise without character is brittle: people stop listening when an expert's actions contradict the values they claim. A specific story about a hard choice, a costly mistake, or a moment of doing the right thing is far more persuasive than a list of qualifications, because it lets the audience see your character at work and infer the rest.

**How to apply.**
- Identify what you genuinely value about your work beyond getting paid.
- Recall hard lessons you learned the painful way and what they taught you.
- Recall times you did the right thing even when it cost you time, money, or status.
- Imagine your project failing and write the one line of consolation you could still honestly say about it.
- Pick the rules of your field you have followed, broken, or rewritten, and explain why.
- Point to concrete actions, not opinions, that demonstrate the values you share with your audience.

## What's My Motivation? {#whats-my-motivation}

**Essence.** Step into the inner life of every key person in your story so you understand what they want and why they would or would not cooperate with you.

**When to reach for it.** When you are mapping a project that depends on the willing participation of users, clients, colleagues, or stakeholders and you cannot afford to misread their motives.

**Why it works.** Anything ambitious requires the cooperation of other people, and cooperation depends on their motivations, not yours. By taking each stakeholder seriously as a character with their own goals, history, and obstacles, you spot conflicts and alliances early. This is the same method actors use to make a character convincing on stage, and it works because the questions are deep enough to surface real motives.

**How to apply.**
- List the key players in your project: clients, users, decision makers, and other stakeholders.
- For each one, work through who they are, where they are, when this is happening, what they want, why they want it, how they will pursue it, and what they must overcome.
- Draw out where their motivations align with yours and where they pull in opposite directions.
- Use those points of cooperation and conflict to build a story about the real motivations driving the situation.

## What's it About? {#whats-it-about}

**Essence.** Force yourself to answer the deceptively simple question of what your work is really about, so you can talk about it without rambling.

**When to reach for it.** When you cannot crisply explain what you are working on, when you need an elevator pitch, or when you suspect you are too close to your own project to see what matters.

**Why it works.** Expertise breeds a curse: the more deeply you know something, the harder it is to remember what an outsider would and would not understand. Forcing yourself through a few simple lenses (what is changing, what is new, who benefits) breaks the curse by reconnecting you to the audience's perspective. The result is a short, confident summary that survives contact with strangers.

**How to apply.**
- Frame it as change: what is shifting in the world, and is your work responding to it or causing it?
- Frame it as new information: what have you noticed that others have not, or what surprised you?
- Frame it as personal benefit: who in the real world is better off because of this, and how?
- Pick the framing that fits your audience and turn it into one short sentence you can say without stumbling.
