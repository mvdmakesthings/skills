# Recipes

Recipe cards stack multiple tactics in a particular order to reach a goal. Each recipe lists which tactics it draws on; load the relevant category reference for the framework on any one of them.

## Stories that Connect {#stories-that-connect}

**Essence.** Build empathy across people — customers, colleagues, teammates — by collecting and reflecting their stories rather than broadcasting your own.

**When to use this recipe.** Use when you're designing a product, leading a team, or navigating a relationship and you suspect you don't actually understand the other side's experience.

**Why this combination.** Empathy is the precondition for good decisions about other humans, and empathy can't be faked from a survey. People reveal what matters to them through stories they tell about their lives and through behavior, not through answers to direct questions. The path to connection is to gather raw narrative material, observe what people actually do, and find the shared human shapes that link your situation to theirs.

**Build the story in this order.**
- Treat conversations as opportunities to receive other people's stories rather than to ask interview questions.
- Watch how people behave and not just what they say — actions reveal what words conceal.
- Hunt for situations and feelings that nearly everyone recognizes so you can meet on shared ground.
- Make ordinary work conversations more narrative-flavored by drawing out specifics and change.
- Turn what you've gathered into recognizable characters and journeys that others see themselves in.

**Tactics this recipe combines.**
- *Story Listening*
- *Abstractions*
- *Universal Stories*
- *Story-ish Conversations*
- *Circle of Life*

## Stories that Convince {#stories-that-convince}

**Essence.** Expertise alone doesn't persuade non-experts — translate years of work into a shape they can follow and a reason to trust your judgment.

**When to use this recipe.** Use when you have deep knowledge or research that a less-informed audience needs to accept, and they're either drowning in your detail or skeptical of your authority.

**Why this combination.** Specialists routinely lose audiences by treating persuasion as an information transfer problem. Audiences have limited working memory, default to skepticism of expertise, and respond to feeling rather than fact density. Combining cognitive load limits with emotional engagement, story-shaped data, and a clear credibility move is what shifts a skeptical listener into a believer.

**Build the story in this order.**
- Strip facts down to the few an audience can actually hold in their head.
- Convey your genuine enthusiasm so the discovery feels alive, not dry.
- Wrap statistics and findings inside narrative shapes instead of bullet lists.
- Establish credibility deliberately rather than assuming the audience already grants it.
- Take the role of a guide alongside the audience, not a distant authority over them.

**Tactics this recipe combines.**
- *Three is the Magic Number*
- *That's Funny*
- *Data Detectives*
- *Trust Me, I'm an Expert*
- *Hero & Guide*

## Stories that Explain {#stories-that-explain}

**Essence.** A strategy document is a story trying to escape — recast direction-setting as a narrative about navigating change so people actually internalize it.

**When to use this recipe.** Use when you need to communicate strategy, vision, or organizational direction and you suspect the document or deck is going unread or unfelt.

**Why this combination.** Strategy is inherently about choosing a path through uncertainty, which is the same skeleton as any story: conflict, stakes, choices, and consequences. When strategy is presented as an abstract list of pillars, it activates none of the neural circuitry that makes humans remember and act. Translating it into a story shape gives stakeholders something they can recall, repeat, and align behind.

**Build the story in this order.**
- Stake out where you stand relative to the disorder in the environment.
- Pick the conflicts you're willing to fight and the ones you're not.
- Make the meaning land — articulate why the strategy matters to the people executing it.
- Show what success would actually look like in vivid, concrete terms.
- Add narrative hooks that make the strategy interesting enough to engage with in the first place.

**Tactics this recipe combines.**
- *Order & Chaos*
- *Good & Evil*
- *What's it About?*
- *Rolls Royce Moment*
- *Story Hooks*

## Stories that Impress {#stories-that-impress}

**Essence.** Presentations fail when they default to corporate-deck conventions — make them feel like a story being told, with attention-grabbing openings, a shape, and a fallback plan.

**When to use this recipe.** Use when you have to stand up and present something important and want it to land as a performance the audience remembers, not as a slide read-along.

**Why this combination.** Audiences in a presentation room are bored, distracted, and skeptical by default. The format itself trains them to expect tedium. Treating the slot like a story changes the contract — you owe them a beginning, middle, and end, sensory engagement through visuals, and a recovery plan if you lose the room. Every one of those moves works because it borrows from theater and oral tradition, which have spent millennia optimizing for attention.

**Build the story in this order.**
- Confirm what you're delivering is actually a story and not just a stack of facts in narrative clothing.
- Apply a simple, repeatable structural beat-pattern so the talk has shape.
- Plant hooks throughout to keep attention from start to finish, not just at the opening.
- Use visuals as illustration and emphasis, never as a substitute for what you're saying.
- Have a contingency move ready for the moment when the room isn't responding.

**Tactics this recipe combines.**
- *Movie Time*
- *Five Ts*
- *Story Hooks*
- *Show and Tell*
- *Cut to the Chase*

## Stories that Lead {#stories-that-lead}

**Essence.** A team's culture is the sum of the stories it tells itself — leaders shape culture by curating which stories get surfaced, told, and remembered.

**When to use this recipe.** Use when you want to build a more cohesive team, transmit shared norms without writing a values document, or make sure hard-won lessons survive turnover.

**Why this combination.** Culture is transmitted through narrative more than through policy. Every team already has stories — about heroes, near-misses, fights, customer moments — but most of them never get spoken aloud, so newcomers learn the culture by accident. Leaders who deliberately collect, examine, and circulate these stories convert lived experience into shared identity.

**Build the story in this order.**
- Learn what genuinely drives each person on the team beyond their job description.
- Reframe shared work as a meaningful, difficult journey rather than a backlog.
- Track the emotional peaks and valleys of projects, not just the deliverables.
- Mine failures for transferable lessons instead of burying them.
- Keep a deliberate library of stories the whole team can draw from.

**Tactics this recipe combines.**
- *Curious Tales*
- *Man in a Hole*
- *Emotional Dashboard*
- *Thoughtful Failures*
- *Story Bank*

## Stories that Motivate {#stories-that-motivate}

**Essence.** To get people to follow you somewhere new, give them both a believable vision of the destination and an honest account of the journey — including the obstacles.

**When to use this recipe.** Use when you need a team, investors, or stakeholders to commit to a meaningful change and you've noticed that a plan alone isn't moving anyone.

**Why this combination.** Asking people to change requires them to absorb risk. They'll only do it if they trust your destination and trust your account of the path. Pure cheerleading triggers skepticism because nothing important is ever truly easy; pure realism without an inspiring goal triggers despair. The motivating pattern is to combine vivid future, honest difficulty, and credible reassurance.

**Build the story in this order.**
- Anchor the audience in the large-scale outcome you're pursuing.
- Explain what's pulling you toward it personally so others can hook into the same motivation.
- Name the real obstacles and adversaries you'll have to work through.
- Acknowledge and de-risk the change you're asking people to accept.
- Set honest expectations about how hard the journey will actually be.

**Tactics this recipe combines.**
- *The Dragon & the City*
- *Drive Stories*
- *Three Great Conflicts*
- *Innovation Curve*
- *No Easy Way*

## Stories that Sell {#stories-that-sell}

**Essence.** Selling is fundamentally a trust transaction, so combine several tactics that establish credibility and relatability rather than relying on numbers or feature lists.

**When to use this recipe.** Use when you need to move a prospect, customer, or buyer toward a purchase decision and a spec sheet or pricing comparison isn't enough to close the gap.

**Why this combination.** Buyers commit when they believe a seller understands their situation and can deliver on a promised future. Both of those beliefs depend on trust, and trust is built through narrative more reliably than through data. Stacking audience research, relatable examples, third-party validation, an aspirational arc, and a tight verbal version creates a layered case that addresses both rational and emotional objections.

**Build the story in this order.**
- First, get clear on who the buyer is and what problem they actually have.
- Show them a story of someone like them who used your offering successfully.
- Reinforce with external evidence — trends, prototypes, or testimonials.
- Frame the buyer's situation using an optimistic arc where they become better off.
- Distill the whole thing into a short, repeatable spoken version.

**Tactics this recipe combines.**
- *Audience Profile*
- *Simple Sales Stories*
- *Social Proof*
- *Rags to Riches*
- *Pitch Perfect*
