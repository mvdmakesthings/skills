# Remote Work Became Permanent. HR Needs to Catch Up.

Most HR departments built their policies, their evaluation frameworks, and their onboarding playbooks around the assumption that people would be in an office five days a week. That assumption broke in 2020 and never came back. The data backs this up: a 2024 Stanford study found that hybrid and fully remote arrangements now cover more than 60% of knowledge workers in the U.S. And companies that forced full return-to-office saw attrition spike among their most experienced people. If your policies still treat remote work as a temporary accommodation, you're solving for a world that stopped existing four years ago.

So what does this mean for HR teams in practice? Compensation models need geographic flexibility baked in, not bolted on as an exception. Performance reviews have to measure output, because measuring hours-in-seat is useless when half your workforce logs in from a different time zone. Onboarding is the one that trips up most organizations. A new hire's first 90 days used to rely on hallway conversations, lunch invitations, and the ambient culture of sitting near their team. All of that has to be designed intentionally now. The companies doing this well assign onboarding buddies, schedule informal video calls that have no agenda, and create async documentation that a new person can actually find without asking six people where it lives.

The bigger shift, though, is cultural. HR professionals who treat remote work as a logistics problem (equipment, VPNs, expense policies) miss the harder question: how do you maintain trust and cohesion when people rarely share a physical space? That requires rethinking management training, communication norms, and what "being available" actually means. It also requires HR to advocate for remote-friendly practices at the leadership level, because executives who came up in office-first cultures often default to what felt normal to them. The organizations that figure this out will hold onto their best people. The ones that don't will keep writing confused LinkedIn posts about why nobody wants to come back.
