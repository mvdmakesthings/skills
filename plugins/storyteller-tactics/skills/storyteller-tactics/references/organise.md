# Organise tactics

How to manage stories across audiences and over time so the right one is ready when needed.

## Audience Profile {#audience-profile}

**Essence.** Invest real time in understanding who your audience is before you decide what story to tell, because a great story aimed at the wrong people lands as noise.

**When to reach for it.** Before any significant pitch, talk, piece of marketing, or campaign, especially when you catch yourself assuming the audience will care about whatever you find interesting.

**Why it works.** Storytellers consistently overestimate how universal their own taste is. The same content that thrills one audience bores another, so the audience must be the starting point, not an afterthought. By profiling who they are, what they need, what they fear, and what they already think about you, you can choose stories that fit them rather than hoping they fit yours.

**How to apply.**
- Capture the basic facts about the audience: who they are, where they are in life, what they do.
- Identify the problems and pains your story can credibly help them with.
- Map their hopes and ambitions so you know which positives to play to.
- Map their fears and objections so you know which negatives to address head-on.
- Note what they already say or assume about your idea so you start from where they are.
- Decide who is best placed to tell the story, given that a satisfied customer often beats a self-promoting expert.
- Be honest about whether the story is yours to tell or whether you are borrowing someone else's lived experience.

## Big, Small, Inside, Outside {#big-small-inside-outside}

**Essence.** Different audiences and situations need different kinds of stories, so sort yours along two axes: how rehearsed they are and who they are for.

**When to reach for it.** When you realise the polished story that works for an external pitch is wrong for a coffee with a colleague, or vice versa, and you need a quick way to pick the right register.

**Why it works.** There is no single best story; there is the right story for the situation. Big stories are rehearsed and delivered to many; small stories are spontaneous and shared one-on-one. Inside stories speak to people who share your context; outside stories speak to those who do not. Mapping your repertoire onto this two-by-two grid makes it obvious where you are well stocked and where you have gaps to fill.

**How to apply.**
- Sort each story by whether it is rehearsed and prepared (big) or quick and spontaneous (small).
- Sort each story by whether it is for colleagues who share your context (inside) or for an outside audience.
- Use small inside stories to shape culture and habits, such as how we do things here and what good looks like.
- Use small outside stories to give external audiences practical examples of what you do and what you stand for.
- Use big inside stories for moments like onboarding, awards, and team celebrations.
- Use big outside stories for high-stakes set pieces like pitches, conferences, and recruitment.

## Story Bank {#story-bank}

**Essence.** Keep a living collection of your best stories along with the context that protects you from telling them in the wrong place or to the wrong person.

**When to reach for it.** Once you have a few good stories worth repeating, especially across a team, and you cannot rely on memory alone to track who you have told what, when, and whether you are allowed to share it.

**Why it works.** Reusable stories are an asset, but they go stale, get repeated to the same audience, or slip out of their original context with embarrassing results. A simple structured store (a notebook or spreadsheet) lets you find the right story for the moment, remember its source and permissions, and refresh it before it ages out. Treat the metadata around the story as seriously as the story itself.

**How to apply.**
- Capture each story with a short title or keyword and a one-line summary of the point.
- Note who would find it useful and in which situations.
- Track who you have already told it to so you do not repeat it on the same audience.
- Record whose story it really is and whether you have permission to retell it.
- Add an expiry sense-check: is it still current, or is it past its sell-by date?
- If it lives online, link out to the underlying posts, articles, or documents for the full story.
