# Function tactics

What job the story has to do: pitch, sell, explain, warm up a room, surface a hidden insight.

## Icebreaker Stories {#icebreaker-stories}

**Essence.** Build a team's storytelling muscle through low-stakes invented exercises before anyone has to tell a high-stakes real story.

**When to reach for it.** Use at the start of a workshop, off-site, or new team formation when you want people to practice narrative thinking without the pressure of real consequences.

**Why it works.** Storytelling is a skill that atrophies in adult professional settings, where people are trained to present data and conclusions rather than scenes and characters. Inviting a team to invent fictional stories around random prompts strips away the fear of getting facts wrong, exercises the imagination, and produces a shared experience that makes later real-stakes storytelling feel familiar.

**How to apply.**
- Use random images as raw material so people aren't biased by familiar topics.
- Have participants impose a simple before-during-after shape on the images they pick.
- Try a polarity exercise where different groups invent love-it and hate-it stories about the same object.
- Push the stories toward sensory detail, emotion, and dramatic stakes.
- Run these solo or with a group — the muscle builds either way.

## Pitch Perfect {#pitch-perfect}

**Essence.** Boil your idea down to a tightly shaped verbal pitch with an emotional arc so it survives being the tenth thing your listener heard today.

**When to reach for it.** Use when you need to make a short, memorable case for an idea — pitching investors, asking for budget, selling a project internally.

**Why it works.** Decisions about whether to back an idea are usually made in seconds, often by someone tired of hearing pitches. A pitch that is just a list of claims competes with every other claim in the listener's day and loses. Wrapping the same content in a problem-promise arc gives the listener emotional contours to remember it by — a low, a hope, a complication, a resolution.

**How to apply.**
- Write a single-sentence top-line version of the idea you can deliver to anyone.
- Answer the three core questions of any short pitch: what problem you solve, how you solve it, and why you can be trusted to deliver.
- Shape the longer version around a curve that moves through problem, opportunity, practical steps, and promised outcome.
- Deliberately accentuate the dips and rises so the pitch has tension rather than reading flat.
- If a visual or document is needed, build it as a story document and not a feature list.

## Simple Sales Stories {#simple-sales-stories}

**Essence.** Convince new buyers by telling them short, relatable stories about existing customers — either someone like them, or someone they look up to.

**When to reach for it.** Use when you're trying to bring new customers on board and abstract product claims aren't moving them.

**Why it works.** Humans constantly take cues from other humans about what's safe and worthwhile. A new buyer is essentially asking 'is this for someone like me, and is it endorsed by someone whose taste I trust?' Sales stories address both questions cheaply. They borrow credibility from existing customers, reduce perceived risk by acting as beta-test evidence, and use familiar narrative shapes to make the case stick.

**How to apply.**
- Tell the story of a customer the prospect resembles, so they see themselves succeeding with the product.
- Tell the story of a customer the prospect admires, so trust flows down from influencer to follower.
- Tell stories of long-tenured customers, returning customers, and well-handled complaint customers to demonstrate durability.
- For each story, be clear on who the listener is and why a particular protagonist would resonate with them.
- Use each story as evidence that the underlying problem is real and the solution actually lands.

## Story-ish Conversations {#story-ish-conversations}

**Essence.** You don't need a stage to use storytelling — pull narrative elements out of everyday meetings and chats to surface insights that would otherwise stay hidden.

**When to reach for it.** Use when ordinary conversations at work feel abstract, circular, or unproductive and you suspect a more concrete framing would unstick them.

**Why it works.** Abstract conversation hides specific knowledge. People know far more than they articulate when prompted only for opinions. Adding lightweight story scaffolding — when did this happen, who was there, what did you feel, what changed — converts vague positions into observable detail. The other person doesn't need to know you're using a technique; they just experience a sharper conversation.

**How to apply.**
- Push for concrete, visual moments instead of generic statements.
- When the conversation stays abstract, ask who-what-where-when questions to ground it in a specific event.
- Probe for emotional contour — what was the strongest feeling, and how did it shift?
- Surface the conflicts people had to work through, because conflicts contain the real insight.
- Look for the moment where new information forced a change of mind, which is usually the actionable nugget.
