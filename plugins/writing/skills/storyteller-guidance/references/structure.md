# Structure tactics

The arc the story follows — fall-and-recover, rise, downfall, voyage, and other shapes.

## Downfall {#downfall}

**Essence.** Tell the story of a once-mighty figure or organization brought down by a hidden internal flaw — both for the catharsis and for the practical lesson about what to avoid.

**When to reach for it.** Use during a post-mortem, retrospective, or cautionary explanation where you want the audience to internalize a specific failure pattern without feeling lectured.

**Why it works.** Stories of the mighty falling combine two emotional engines: a guilty pleasure in seeing the powerful humbled, and the protective instinct to avoid making the same mistake. The structure works because it isolates a single fatal flaw — hubris, dishonesty, a strategic blindness — and traces a credible line from that flaw to ruin. When applied to your own past projects or a competitor's, it makes the lesson stick more than any list of best practices.

**How to apply.**
- Identify the protagonist who was once dominant or admired.
- Name the specific hidden flaw — internal, not bad luck — that will undo them.
- Trace how the flaw produced choices that looked fine in the moment.
- Show the collapse as the inevitable consequence of those choices.
- Use the story to draw out the lesson the audience should now apply.

## Epic Fail {#epic-fail}

**Essence.** When something goes wrong, choose deliberately which type of failure story you tell, because the frame you pick determines the lesson everyone takes away.

**When to reach for it.** Use after a setback, a missed launch, a botched project, or any retrospective where you and your audience need to make sense of what just happened.

**Why it works.** Research on failed ventures shows people reach for a handful of recurring narratives to explain disaster: I broke it, we got cocky, someone betrayed us, the system failed, the times were against us, or an enemy did this to us. Each frame puts the spotlight in a different place — on the self, the team, an individual, the system, the era, or a villain — and each has trade-offs in what it teaches and what it dodges. Picking the frame consciously, rather than slipping into the most convenient one, lets you control the moral of the story and the resilience that follows.

**How to apply.**
- Frame the failure as personal accountability and growth when you want to model humility and learning.
- Frame it as collective overconfidence when the lesson is about hubris and team blind spots.
- Frame it as betrayal by someone close when the failure was a relationship or trust breakdown — but expect collateral damage.
- Frame it as a system or process breakdown when you want to depersonalise the cause and fix the machinery.
- Frame it as a sign of the times when context and broader forces meaningfully shaped the outcome.
- Frame it as deliberate sabotage by an antagonist only when there really was hostile intent worth naming.

## Five Ts {#five-ts}

**Essence.** Keep a small checklist of story ingredients in your back pocket so that when someone unexpectedly asks for a story you can assemble one on the spot.

**When to reach for it.** Use when you need to be ready to improvise a credible story under pressure — a sudden question in a meeting, an interview, a panel, or a hallway conversation.

**Why it works.** Off-the-cuff storytelling fails because we reach for impressive material and end up performing instead of telling. A short prompt list reminds you that any decent story needs a sequence in time, a moment where something turned, real emotional friction, a moral choice, and a lesson — and that's all you need to start. Working from those prompts keeps the telling natural rather than rehearsed, which is exactly what audiences trust.

**How to apply.**
- Set the sequence in time — a beginning state, what changed, and what resulted, even in three short sentences.
- Pinpoint a turning point — a realisation, decision, or pivot that the rest of the story hinges on.
- Surface tension — the worst moment, the anxiety, or the conflict that gives the story stakes.
- Include a temptation — a moment where the easy or dishonest path was on offer and someone chose otherwise.
- Land a teachable moment — what the experience taught you, framed as a takeaway your listener can use.

## Happy Ever Afters {#happy-ever-afters}

**Essence.** Give your story a satisfying resolution that shows a meaningful change in your protagonist, because audiences are wired to want a payoff that feels earned.

**When to reach for it.** Use when crafting the closing beat of a pitch, presentation, or case study so the audience leaves with a sense of completion rather than ambiguity.

**Why it works.** Endings carry disproportionate weight in how we remember stories. The deepest satisfactions are not just outcomes but transformations: someone grew up, someone found belonging, someone did the right thing despite cost. Each of these resolutions taps a different universal hunger, so picking the right one for your protagonist and audience determines how the whole story lands. Even if real life is messier, holding out the hope of one of these endings lifts the audience and makes them root for the journey.

**How to apply.**
- Decide whether your protagonist grows up — what life-stage transition or new maturity have they reached, and how did you help.
- Decide whether they find a home, love, or earned respect — how the place they return to is different because of who they've become.
- Decide whether they do the right thing under pressure — what bad thing was put right, at what cost, and what made the moral choice possible.
- Work backwards from the chosen ending so the body of the story makes that resolution feel inevitable rather than tacked on.

## Innovation Curve {#innovation-curve}

**Essence.** Different segments of an audience tolerate different amounts of risk — calibrate the story you tell about your idea to where each listener sits on the adoption curve.

**When to reach for it.** Use when you're selling something novel and noticing that the same pitch lands with some people and gets rejected by others.

**Why it works.** Risk tolerance is a stable trait of audience segments, not a universal property. Pioneers are excited by novelty itself; mainstream buyers want proof other people have already gone first; traditional buyers want to feel they're the last safe holdouts joining a sure thing. The story stays roughly the same, but the framing, evidence, and emotional appeal must change based on what each segment fears and hopes for.

**How to apply.**
- Identify which adoption segment you're talking to — innovator, pioneer, early or late mainstream, or traditional.
- For each segment, name the specific hope you can speak to and the specific fear you must address.
- Lead with novelty and excitement for pioneers and early adopters; lead with safety and ubiquity for traditionals.
- Use different evidence types — process and prototypes for early, full readiness and recommendations for late.
- Don't pitch a traditional buyer like a pioneer or you'll be rejected as reckless.

## Man in a Hole {#man-in-a-hole}

**Essence.** Use the classic fall-and-recover arc — life is fine, something knocks the protagonist down, they learn something in the dark, and they climb back stronger.

**When to reach for it.** Use when you want to make a point about resilience, lessons learned, or a recovery from adversity — including pandemics, layoffs, failed launches, or personal setbacks.

**Why it works.** This arc is universally legible because it mirrors lived experience. Audiences accept that protagonists can be knocked down, and they want to see what gets found in the dark. The structure works because the descent makes the recovery meaningful — without the hole, the climb has no weight. The wisdom acquired during the low point is what justifies telling the story at all.

**How to apply.**
- Open in a settled state — not utopia, but a recognizable normal with some latent gap.
- Trigger the fall with an external shock or an ignored warning.
- Spend real time in the crisis, because that's where the audience finds the lesson.
- Show the recovery as deliberate application of what was learned, not luck.
- Land in a better place that is wiser and more durable than the original normal.

## No Easy Way {#no-easy-way}

**Essence.** Use an extended up-and-down arc that includes early wins, setbacks, near-disasters, and hard-won recoveries — promising-nothing-is-free is more credible than promising-nothing-is-hard.

**When to reach for it.** Use when a simple optimistic story would sound suspicious because the audience knows real journeys aren't smooth — such as transformation stories, change initiatives, or product roadmaps.

**Why it works.** Smooth success stories trigger skeptical pattern-matching with marketing fluff. Audiences trust narratives that include setbacks because real lives include setbacks. A story arc that traces a problem to early success to a serious crisis and finally to a tested recovery feels honest, and the honesty bleeds into trust about whatever you're advocating. Without showing the hard middle, you forfeit the credibility that makes the ending earned.

**How to apply.**
- Begin with a bad place where danger is real or potential is wasted.
- Show an early opportunity that produces visible progress.
- Introduce a setback that reveals a weakness or surfaces opposition.
- Let it get genuinely bad — to the point where the audience wonders if it was worth starting.
- Show recovery rooted in what was learned in the dark, and end in a better place than the start.

## Pride & Fall {#pride-and-fall}

**Essence.** Use the cautionary descent arc — a confident rise that ignores warnings and ends worse than it began — to address a skeptical audience's suspicion that things sound too good to be true.

**When to reach for it.** Use when you suspect your audience is mentally rehearsing the story of your project's failure, and you need to acknowledge that risk before pivoting toward credibility.

**Why it works.** Skeptics aren't moved by more optimism; they're moved when you demonstrate that you've considered failure. Telling the failure-arc version of your own project — in the abstract or as a competitor's cautionary tale — proves you've thought about what could go wrong. Once that doubt is acknowledged, the audience is willing to consider why your version is different. The arc itself is among the oldest narrative shapes for exactly this reason.

**How to apply.**
- Identify the bad place your protagonist starts in.
- Show the prideful rise — what looks like progress but contains the seed of failure.
- Surface the warnings that get ignored along the way.
- Let the fall happen as a consequence of those ignored warnings.
- End in a worse place than the starting point, then use it as a setup for a more honest story you can tell about your own work.

## Rags to Riches {#rags-to-riches}

**Essence.** Use the optimistic ascent arc where a hero with hidden value rises from a bad situation — but remember the prize is the value being recognized, not literal wealth.

**When to reach for it.** Use when you want to tell an aspirational success story — a customer transformation, a founder origin, a product's underdog rise — without sounding hollow.

**Why it works.** Audiences are drawn to ascent stories because they want to believe their own latent value will be recognized. The arc works because it withholds reward until the protagonist's hidden quality has been tested and proven. Telling it badly — promising money or status as the payoff — flattens it into a get-rich-quick pitch and triggers skepticism. Told well, the recognition of intrinsic worth is what the audience actually craves.

**How to apply.**
- Begin with the hero in a bad place, but with a quality nobody else can yet see.
- Introduce a trigger that gives them a chance to act on what they have.
- Show the struggle — they may get help, but the climb is fundamentally their own.
- Resolve with deserved recognition of the quality that was there all along.
- If telling someone else's story, ask whether you're the hero or the guide and play your role honestly.

## Voyage & Return {#voyage-and-return}

**Essence.** Frame the work as a round trip into an unfamiliar world, because audiences are hooked by the promise that the traveller — and home itself — will be changed by the journey.

**When to reach for it.** Use when describing an innovation, an exploration, or any project that takes someone out of their comfort zone and brings them back transformed.

**Why it works.** The voyage structure works because it mirrors how change actually feels: safety becomes restless, the unknown looks dangerous and seductive, the new world teaches you things you couldn't learn at home, and the return reframes everything familiar. You can cast yourself as the traveller or, more powerfully, cast the customer as the traveller and yourself as their guide. Either way, the arc lets the audience feel risk and resolution without leaving the room.

**How to apply.**
- Sketch the starting home — safe, known, and ultimately too small for what your protagonist needs.
- Show what pulls them outward — a desire, a force, or an accident that initiates the journey.
- Describe the strange new world — its threats, its strangeness, and the lessons available there.
- Mark the price of progress — what is gained, what is given up, and what nearly went wrong.
- Bring them back changed — how home looks different now and how they'll make it better with what they learned.
