# Explore tactics

How to find raw story material in your work, your research, and your customers.

## Abstractions {#abstractions}

**Essence.** Watch what people actually do rather than relying on what they say they do, because real understanding lives in behaviour, not explanation.

**When to reach for it.** When researching users, customers, or any audience whose lived experience matters more than their tidy after-the-fact account of it.

**Why it works.** When humans describe their own behaviour, they typically deliver a rationalised, abstracted version that smooths over the messy details. The deeper layers (the actions, the imitation, the rituals, the stories people tell themselves) carry far more useful information than the polished explanation on top. Observation peels back the layers in a way that interviews on their own rarely can.

**How to apply.**
- Watch people in action and notice what they are physically doing.
- Notice who they are copying or modelling their behaviour on.
- Spot the patterns, routines, and unwritten rules they follow.
- Listen for the stories they tell themselves about why they do this.
- Compare their reasoning to what you actually observed, and treat any gap as a clue.

## Data Detectives {#data-detectives}

**Essence.** Wrap your data in story so people actually remember it, because numbers and charts decay fast in memory but a narrative built around them lasts.

**When to reach for it.** Use when you've done research or analysis and need stakeholders to retain not just the headline finding but the reasoning and the implications.

**Why it works.** Most audiences struggle with data even when it's well-visualised. They will, however, remember a handful of facts if those facts are embedded in a story — a story that zooms in from pattern to vivid example, or one that mimics an investigation, or one that honestly acknowledges what the data can't say. Each style of data story plays a different role: one explains, one persuades, one builds trust by showing your epistemic limits.

**How to apply.**
- Tell a zoom story — start from the broad trend, drop into a single vivid example that typifies it, and connect them with phrasing that says here's the pattern and here's what it looks like up close.
- Tell an investigation story — frame the question as the body on the floor, your sources as the search for evidence, the surprising finding as the vital clue, and the implication as the solution.
- Tell a humble story — acknowledge how the data could mislead, what alternative readings exist, and what matters that simply can't be measured.
- Pick the style that matches what your audience needs — comprehension, conviction, or trust — and don't try to do all three at once.

## Emotional Dashboard {#emotional-dashboard}

**Essence.** Track strong emotions — yours, your users', your stakeholders' — because they are the surest signal of where a real story is waiting to be told.

**When to reach for it.** Use during research or self-reflection when you need to find the moment in a project, journey, or experience that's worth telling a story about.

**Why it works.** Emotions function like gauges on a dashboard. They flash red when we're being blocked from a goal, glow green when things are going to plan, and shift constantly as we read other people. Because emotions bind groups together — and tear them apart — moments of strong feeling are reliable markers of decisive change. If you can locate a strong emotion at a specific moment, you've almost certainly found a story; the rest is just turning the cause, the realisation, the original goal, and the lesson learned into sentences.

**How to apply.**
- List positive emotions you've felt in the project (happiness, confidence, love, delight, trust, pride, excitement, joy, conviction).
- List negative emotions (sadness, fear, hurt, disgust, distrust, shame, boredom, anger, hate).
- Take each strong emotion and turn it into a story sentence: I felt X, when I realised Y, because I wanted Z, and so I learned W.
- Repeat the same exercise from your customer's or user's vantage point — what is the strongest feeling they're experiencing, and how does your work move that needle?
- Build the most charged moment into a scene that anchors the larger narrative.

## Social Proof {#social-proof}

**Essence.** When trust is the bottleneck, borrow it by showing that other credible people are already moving in the direction you are recommending.

**When to reach for it.** Before a pitch, sales conversation, or persuasion moment where the audience is hesitant and looking around to see whether anyone else has taken the leap.

**Why it works.** In uncertain situations, humans take cues from each other rather than reasoning from first principles. Evidence that other people are acting, especially people the audience identifies with, lowers the perceived risk of joining in. A specific story about real behaviour persuades far more than a generic claim, because it lets the listener picture themselves in the same position.

**How to apply.**
- Show trend data that lots of people are already moving a certain way, ideally with an early adopter worth watching.
- Highlight prototypes, pilots, and trials and describe how people actually behaved, not just what they said.
- Gather testimonials that reach past bland praise by asking about one specific thing the person loved, the moment they knew it was the right call, and how they would persuade a friend to try it.
- Zoom out to the broad pattern and then zoom in on a single vivid example.

## Story Listening {#story-listening}

**Essence.** Treat listening to other people's stories as a discipline in its own right — at least equal to telling — because the wisdom you need to be a credible storyteller almost always lives in someone else's experience first.

**When to reach for it.** Use during user research, customer interviews, or any moment when you need real material for a story rather than imagined material.

**Why it works.** Researchers who study experts under pressure — pilots, firefighters, nurses, soldiers — learn the most by listening for the moments where the expert learned something the hard way. The same approach works for understanding users and stakeholders. You're not gathering data points; you're hunting for the moments people return to, the strong emotions they attach, the decisions they made at branching points, and the gap between novice and expert thinking. Done well, story listening produces the raw material for stories that are true rather than invented.

**How to apply.**
- First pass — identify the memorable story by asking why it sticks and what strong emotion is attached.
- Second pass — build the basic timeline of what they were trying to achieve and what actually happened.
- Third pass — add the key decisions, the options they considered, and the roads not taken.
- Fourth pass — identify the expertise by asking where a novice would have gone wrong and what someone with different expertise might have missed.
- Convert the teachable moments into the spine of the story you'll tell back.

## That's Funny {#thats-funny}

**Essence.** Hunt for the small moments in your work that made you laugh, raise an eyebrow, or do a double-take, because they are the seeds of the best stories.

**When to reach for it.** While reviewing a project or research material for things worth telling, especially when the obvious headlines feel flat and you suspect a richer angle is hiding in the odd details.

**Why it works.** Surprise is the brain noticing that its model of the world is wrong. Whether the surprise is amusing or unsettling, it signals that something interesting is happening just below the surface. By treating those moments as evidence rather than noise, you give yourself raw material that already carries built-in tension and audience interest.

**How to apply.**
- Replay the project and flag anything that made you snort, smile, or pause in confusion.
- Treat amusement as a clue that a mental model just shifted and ask what changed.
- Treat the strange or unexpected as a clue that your data or assumptions may be wrong somewhere.
- Look for coincidences that hint at a connection you have not made explicit yet.
- When people are acting out of character, ask what their behaviour says about your assumptions about them.

## Thoughtful Failures {#thoughtful-failures}

**Essence.** Treat your most considered failures as material worth talking about openly, because they're a richer source of insight — and an easier audience-builder — than your wins.

**When to reach for it.** Use when reflecting on a project that didn't land, or when deciding whether to share an experiment that didn't pay off the way you hoped.

**Why it works.** People rarely fail through carelessness; most failures arrive after a lot of thought and reasonable bets. Companies that explicitly reward this kind of intelligent risk-taking know that the lessons inside a thoughtful failure are usually more valuable than the lessons inside a fluky success. The only thing that turns a failure into a tragedy is concealing it or refusing to learn from it — so the act of telling the story is itself what redeems the outcome.

**How to apply.**
- Map out what went wrong from many angles, focusing on decisions and process rather than individuals.
- Sort the lessons into goals you were chasing, assumptions you held, new insights you gained, and skills you still need.
- Ask what a future version of the team should do differently next time.
- Identify who else needs this lesson so the same mistake does not happen elsewhere.
- Shape the experience into a teachable narrative with a clear arc of struggle and learning.
