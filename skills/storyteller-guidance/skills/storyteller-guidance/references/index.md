# Card index

Every card in the deck with a one-line essence. Use this to find a specific tactic by name or to scan a category quickly. Each entry links to the full framework in its category file.

## By category

### system

- **Story Building System** — A diagnostic table of contents that routes you to the right category of storytelling tactic by asking a sequence of yes/no questions about where you're stuck.

### recipe

- [Stories that Connect](recipes.md#stories-that-connect) — Build empathy across people — customers, colleagues, teammates — by collecting and reflecting their stories rather than broadcasting your own.
- [Stories that Convince](recipes.md#stories-that-convince) — Expertise alone doesn't persuade non-experts — translate years of work into a shape they can follow and a reason to trust your judgment.
- [Stories that Explain](recipes.md#stories-that-explain) — A strategy document is a story trying to escape — recast direction-setting as a narrative about navigating change so people actually internalize it.
- [Stories that Impress](recipes.md#stories-that-impress) — Presentations fail when they default to corporate-deck conventions — make them feel like a story being told, with attention-grabbing openings, a shape, and a fallback plan.
- [Stories that Lead](recipes.md#stories-that-lead) — A team's culture is the sum of the stories it tells itself — leaders shape culture by curating which stories get surfaced, told, and remembered.
- [Stories that Motivate](recipes.md#stories-that-motivate) — To get people to follow you somewhere new, give them both a believable vision of the destination and an honest account of the journey — including the obstacles.
- [Stories that Sell](recipes.md#stories-that-sell) — Selling is fundamentally a trust transaction, so combine several tactics that establish credibility and relatability rather than relying on numbers or feature lists.

### Concept

- [Circle of Life](concept.md#circle-of-life) — Tell your story through the universal life-stage archetypes — child, adult, parent — because audiences recognise themselves in the roles people occupy as they leave home, find a wider world, and build a new home of their own.
- [Curious Tales](concept.md#curious-tales) — Surface the small things that genuinely fascinate someone — what surprises them, scares them, absorbs them, makes them envious, or makes them give things up — to reveal what really drives them.
- [Good & Evil](concept.md#good-and-evil) — Lift your story by anchoring it to a moral conflict, either a clean right-versus-wrong battle or, more powerfully, a collision between two competing rights or two unavoidable wrongs.
- [Order & Chaos](concept.md#order-and-chaos) — Show that your work either tames a chaotic situation or shakes loose one that has become too rigid, casting yourself or your user as the hero who finds the right balance.
- [Rules, Cheats & Rebels](concept.md#rules-cheats-and-rebels) — Name a rule, then show someone breaking it — the audience cannot look away from a transgression and will follow you to find out whether the breaker is punished or vindicated.
- [Secrets & Puzzles](concept.md#secrets-and-puzzles) — Open with a hidden thing the audience wants revealed or a contradiction they want resolved, because an open information gap is one of the strongest attention hooks there is.
- [Shock of the Old](concept.md#shock-of-the-old) — Mine objects and practices that have survived for generations for the deep insights they encode, because anything that has lasted that long is solving a problem we still have.
- [The Dragon & the City](concept.md#the-dragon-and-the-city) — Recast your project as a city threatened by a dragon, with options of fleeing, fortifying, or attacking — a primal frame that makes ordinary business choices feel urgent and consequential.
- [Three Great Conflicts](concept.md#three-great-conflicts) — Pick the right fight at the heart of your story — your hero against nature, against other people, or against themselves — because every memorable story turns on one of these three.
- [Universal Stories](concept.md#universal-stories) — Build your story on the human traits everyone shares across cultures, because those universals are the most reliable foundation for an audience to recognise themselves in.

### Explore

- [Abstractions](explore.md#abstractions) — Watch what people actually do rather than relying on what they say they do, because real understanding lives in behaviour, not explanation.
- [Data Detectives](explore.md#data-detectives) — Wrap your data in story so people actually remember it, because numbers and charts decay fast in memory but a narrative built around them lasts.
- [Emotional Dashboard](explore.md#emotional-dashboard) — Track strong emotions — yours, your users', your stakeholders' — because they are the surest signal of where a real story is waiting to be told.
- [Social Proof](explore.md#social-proof) — When trust is the bottleneck, borrow it by showing that other credible people are already moving in the direction you are recommending.
- [Story Listening](explore.md#story-listening) — Treat listening to other people's stories as a discipline in its own right — at least equal to telling — because the wisdom you need to be a credible storyteller almost always lives in someone else's experience first.
- [That's Funny](explore.md#thats-funny) — Hunt for the small moments in your work that made you laugh, raise an eyebrow, or do a double-take, because they are the seeds of the best stories.
- [Thoughtful Failures](explore.md#thoughtful-failures) — Treat your most considered failures as material worth talking about openly, because they're a richer source of insight — and an easier audience-builder — than your wins.

### Character

- [Cut to the Chase](character.md#cut-to-the-chase) — When your audience is drifting, abandon your build-up and jump straight to the moment of action, emotion, or insight that matters most.
- [Drive Stories](character.md#drive-stories) — Build a motivating team culture by collecting real stories of when people felt autonomous, skilled, and connected to purpose, plus the times they did not.
- [Hero & Guide](character.md#hero-and-guide) — Cast the audience as the hero of the story and yourself as the experienced guide who helps them get where they want to go.
- [Trust Me, I'm an Expert](character.md#trust-me-im-an-expert) — Earn the audience's trust by telling a story in which your values and judgement showed up in action, not by reciting your credentials.
- [What's My Motivation?](character.md#whats-my-motivation) — Step into the inner life of every key person in your story so you understand what they want and why they would or would not cooperate with you.
- [What's it About?](character.md#whats-it-about) — Force yourself to answer the deceptively simple question of what your work is really about, so you can talk about it without rambling.

### Function

- [Icebreaker Stories](function.md#icebreaker-stories) — Build a team's storytelling muscle through low-stakes invented exercises before anyone has to tell a high-stakes real story.
- [Pitch Perfect](function.md#pitch-perfect) — Boil your idea down to a tightly shaped verbal pitch with an emotional arc so it survives being the tenth thing your listener heard today.
- [Simple Sales Stories](function.md#simple-sales-stories) — Convince new buyers by telling them short, relatable stories about existing customers — either someone like them, or someone they look up to.
- [Story-ish Conversations](function.md#story-ish-conversations) — You don't need a stage to use storytelling — pull narrative elements out of everyday meetings and chats to surface insights that would otherwise stay hidden.

### Structure

- [Downfall](structure.md#downfall) — Tell the story of a once-mighty figure or organization brought down by a hidden internal flaw — both for the catharsis and for the practical lesson about what to avoid.
- [Epic Fail](structure.md#epic-fail) — When something goes wrong, choose deliberately which type of failure story you tell, because the frame you pick determines the lesson everyone takes away.
- [Five Ts](structure.md#five-ts) — Keep a small checklist of story ingredients in your back pocket so that when someone unexpectedly asks for a story you can assemble one on the spot.
- [Happy Ever Afters](structure.md#happy-ever-afters) — Give your story a satisfying resolution that shows a meaningful change in your protagonist, because audiences are wired to want a payoff that feels earned.
- [Innovation Curve](structure.md#innovation-curve) — Different segments of an audience tolerate different amounts of risk — calibrate the story you tell about your idea to where each listener sits on the adoption curve.
- [Man in a Hole](structure.md#man-in-a-hole) — Use the classic fall-and-recover arc — life is fine, something knocks the protagonist down, they learn something in the dark, and they climb back stronger.
- [No Easy Way](structure.md#no-easy-way) — Use an extended up-and-down arc that includes early wins, setbacks, near-disasters, and hard-won recoveries — promising-nothing-is-free is more credible than promising-nothing-is-hard.
- [Pride & Fall](structure.md#pride-and-fall) — Use the cautionary descent arc — a confident rise that ignores warnings and ends worse than it began — to address a skeptical audience's suspicion that things sound too good to be true.
- [Rags to Riches](structure.md#rags-to-riches) — Use the optimistic ascent arc where a hero with hidden value rises from a bad situation — but remember the prize is the value being recognized, not literal wealth.
- [Voyage & Return](structure.md#voyage-and-return) — Frame the work as a round trip into an unfamiliar world, because audiences are hooked by the promise that the traveller — and home itself — will be changed by the journey.

### Style

- [Leave it Out!](style.md#leave-it-out) — Trust the audience to fill in the gaps; the part you withhold is often more powerful than the part you tell.
- [Movie Time](style.md#movie-time) — For every abstract idea you want to land, give the audience a concrete scene their imagination can actually play in their head.
- [Rolls Royce Moment](style.md#rolls-royce-moment) — Find one tiny, vivid, sensory detail that stands in for everything you want the audience to believe about your product or idea.
- [Show & Tell](style.md#show-and-tell) — Make slides and words pull in different but complementary directions so the audience stays engaged with both you and what is on screen.
- [Story Hooks](style.md#story-hooks) — Open with a sharp, curiosity-baiting hook that makes people want to find out what happens next, otherwise the rest of your story never gets read.
- [Three is the Magic Number](style.md#three-is-the-magic-number) — Group the most important parts of your story into threes, because three is the smallest pattern your audience can hold, feel, and remember.

### Organise

- [Audience Profile](organise.md#audience-profile) — Invest real time in understanding who your audience is before you decide what story to tell, because a great story aimed at the wrong people lands as noise.
- [Big, Small, Inside, Outside](organise.md#big-small-inside-outside) — Different audiences and situations need different kinds of stories, so sort yours along two axes: how rehearsed they are and who they are for.
- [Story Bank](organise.md#story-bank) — Keep a living collection of your best stories along with the context that protects you from telling them in the wrong place or to the wrong person.
