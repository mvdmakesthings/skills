# Style tactics

How to deliver the story: hooks, threes, vivid moments, withheld details, slide rhythm.

## Leave it Out! {#leave-it-out}

**Essence.** Trust the audience to fill in the gaps; the part you withhold is often more powerful than the part you tell.

**When to reach for it.** When your draft feels overstuffed or obvious, when you suspect you are spelling out things the audience would enjoy figuring out, or when you want to spark curiosity rather than satisfy it.

**Why it works.** The mind is uncomfortable with gaps and will rush to fill them, which is exactly why suggestion is so much more engaging than full description. By implying instead of explaining, you make the audience co-author of the story, and their version is more vivid than anything you could have written. As a side benefit, what they fill in reveals their assumptions back to you.

**How to apply.**
- List what your audience is expecting to hear and consider cutting some of it.
- Hint at what has been left out so the gap is felt rather than missed.
- Plant questions they will want to answer themselves, such as what happens next or how we got here.
- Leave them wanting more rather than fully satisfied.
- After they fill in the gap, check whether their version matches yours or reveals a difference worth knowing.

## Movie Time {#movie-time}

**Essence.** For every abstract idea you want to land, give the audience a concrete scene their imagination can actually play in their head.

**When to reach for it.** When your subject matter is full of jargon, theory, or data, and you can feel listeners glazing over because there is nothing for their mind's eye to look at.

**Why it works.** Brains process language by simulating it visually. Words like sustainability or accessibility do not start any movie playing, so they slide past. A short scene with action, characters, and stakes hijacks the same neural machinery the audience uses for real experience, which makes the message both clearer and more memorable.

**How to apply.**
- Pick the abstract concept you want to convey and decide what scene best illustrates it.
- Set the action: where are we, what is happening, what happens next?
- Set the emotion: who is involved, what is at stake, how does it feel?
- Land the meaning: what changed, why does it matter, what should we think or do now?
- Self-check by asking, at the end of every paragraph, what movie is now playing in the audience's head.

## Rolls Royce Moment {#rolls-royce-moment}

**Essence.** Find one tiny, vivid, sensory detail that stands in for everything you want the audience to believe about your product or idea.

**When to reach for it.** When you have done the research and have impressive facts, but they are not landing emotionally and you need a single image that makes the quality feel real.

**Why it works.** Abstract qualities like quality or care are forgettable; sensory specifics are sticky. A small, surprising detail acts as evidence the audience can replay in their imagination, and it tells them you did the homework needed to find it. Done well, one well-chosen image carries more conviction than a page of claims.

**How to apply.**
- Make the moment vivid: pick a detail your audience can see, hear, taste, smell, or feel.
- Make sure it is exemplary: that one small fact should stand in for the whole story about what your work does well.
- Make sure it is relatable: anchor it to something the audience already knows so they can feel the contrast.
- Do the digging needed to find this detail; the homework is the work.

## Show & Tell {#show-and-tell}

**Essence.** Make slides and words pull in different but complementary directions so the audience stays engaged with both you and what is on screen.

**When to reach for it.** When preparing a presentation, deck, or any visual talk where the temptation is either to read your slides aloud or to ignore them while talking about something else.

**Why it works.** When the words you say match the words on screen, the audience reads ahead and tunes you out. When the words stray too far from the visuals, the audience gets confused and gives up. The sweet spot is a rhythm where the slide refreshes attention with a new image, and your voice tells the audience what about that image to notice. The two channels alternate like clothes on a washing line, each one pulling attention back to the other.

**How to apply.**
- Decide what the slide is for: a fresh focus, not a script.
- Use the visual to introduce something the audience cannot get from your words alone.
- Use your voice to say what the visual means, not what it literally shows.
- When the audience has absorbed the current pairing, swap in a new image to refresh attention and repeat the pattern.
- Avoid mirroring the slide text in your speech; that combination puts everyone to sleep.

## Story Hooks {#story-hooks}

**Essence.** Open with a sharp, curiosity-baiting hook that makes people want to find out what happens next, otherwise the rest of your story never gets read.

**When to reach for it.** Whenever the first few seconds will decide whether the audience keeps reading or listening, such as an email subject line, a talk opener, an article lead, or a video thumbnail.

**Why it works.** Attention is the scarcest resource you are competing for. A hook works by opening a curiosity gap (a question your audience now needs to close) or by promising something they could not get anywhere else. Several reliable hook patterns exploit different cognitive itches, and choosing the right one for your audience matters more than the rest of the writing.

**How to apply.**
- Open with a question that makes the audience need an answer.
- Frame the topic in an unexpected way, such as how to fail at something everyone wants to succeed at.
- Lean into irony when reality contradicts what it should be.
- Use direct, personal language (you, we, us) so the audience feels addressed.
- Promise specific knowledge they did not have a minute ago.
- Tease a secret, a reveal, or something hidden that they will want let in on.

## Three is the Magic Number {#three-is-the-magic-number}

**Essence.** Group the most important parts of your story into threes, because three is the smallest pattern your audience can hold, feel, and remember.

**When to reach for it.** Any time you need an idea to stick: a key message, a structure for a talk, a list of takeaways, or a build that needs to feel both complete and digestible.

**Why it works.** Working memory is tiny, and two items feel arbitrary while four feel like a list. Three is the smallest number that registers as a deliberate pattern, which is why it shows up everywhere from fairy tales to political speeches. Three also gives you room to use repetition, contrast, or escalation without dragging.

**How to apply.**
- Use a three-fold repetition to signal that a detail is deliberate and worth paying attention to.
- Use a setup-setup-twist pattern, where two parallel beats build an expectation that the third breaks.
- Use two extremes plus a middle ground to argue for the balanced answer.
- Cut anything that does not earn its place inside one of the three slots.
