# Concept tactics

Why a story is needed and what shape its core idea should take.

## Circle of Life {#circle-of-life}

**Essence.** Tell your story through the universal life-stage archetypes — child, adult, parent — because audiences recognise themselves in the roles people occupy as they leave home, find a wider world, and build a new home of their own.

**When to reach for it.** Use when you want a story to feel deeply relatable across audiences by mapping your characters or users onto stages and roles everyone has lived through.

**Why it works.** Some character patterns sit so deeply in shared experience that we recognise them instantly. We were all children — innocent, playful, naive. Many of us become adults — adventurous, clever, sometimes rebellious. Many of us become parents — wise, supportive, sometimes domineering. Each archetype has a positive and a negative face, and stories gain emotional traction when they place a person at one stage, show the role they're playing, and acknowledge both the strengths and the shadow of that role.

**How to apply.**
- Identify which life-stage your protagonist or user is in and which archetypal role they're occupying.
- Decide whether the story is about an internal conflict between the good and bad sides of their current archetype.
- Or decide whether it's about a transition — a rite of passage from one archetype to the next, and your role in helping them make it.
- Or decide whether it's about the friction between the archetype they're supposed to play and the one they actually want to play.
- Anchor specific scenes in recognisable life moments so the universality lands without becoming generic.

## Curious Tales {#curious-tales}

**Essence.** Surface the small things that genuinely fascinate someone — what surprises them, scares them, absorbs them, makes them envious, or makes them give things up — to reveal what really drives them.

**When to reach for it.** Use during research, interviews, or team-building when you need to understand what motivates a person more honestly than their stated goals will tell you.

**Why it works.** Curiosity is involuntary and personal — you can't will yourself to find something interesting. Because of that, the things people actually pay attention to give away their underlying drives more reliably than the goals they tell you about. Asking what someone is currently noticing (with hope), what makes them uneasy (with caution), where they lose track of time (flow), who they envy (and what that envy reveals), and what they've sacrificed for a path they chose, builds up a portrait of motivation you can turn into a believable character story.

**How to apply.**
- Ask what new or unusual thing they keep noticing with a sense of possibility — eureka moments and slow-burn intrigues both count.
- Ask what new or unusual thing makes them uneasy — what feels off, threatening, or potentially harmful.
- Ask when they last lost themselves in absorbing work — what they were doing, who they were with, and what that flow says about their goals.
- Ask who they envy in a healthy, motivating way — not luxury-coveting, but seeing someone else do the work they wish they were doing.
- Ask what they've sacrificed to follow their chosen path — what was given up, what it cost, and what that choice reveals.
- Stitch the answers together into a character study you can use to shape the rest of the story.

## Good & Evil {#good-and-evil}

**Essence.** Lift your story by anchoring it to a moral conflict, either a clean right-versus-wrong battle or, more powerfully, a collision between two competing rights or two unavoidable wrongs.

**When to reach for it.** Use when your story feels morally neutral and you want it to matter — or when the situation is genuinely complex and you need to honour that complexity rather than flatten it.

**Why it works.** Six recurring moral conflicts show up across cultures: care versus harm, fairness versus cheating, freedom versus oppression, authority versus subversion, loyalty versus betrayal, and purity versus corruption. Simple stories pick a side and rally the audience. The richer move, following Hegel's idea that true tragedy is right colliding with right, is to put two genuine goods or two unavoidable evils in tension and let the audience feel the bind. That complexity is what gives serious storytelling — including the best prestige television — its hold on us.

**How to apply.**
- Identify which of the recurring moral axes your work touches.
- For a simple version, pick a clear side on one axis and make the opponent's position visible enough to be defeated.
- For a complex version, find two values in genuine tension and show your protagonist forced to balance them or choose the lesser evil.
- Avoid manufacturing false moral stakes; if the conflict isn't there, this tactic will feel hollow.
- Use the moral question as the engine of the story — the resolution of the conflict should be the climax.

## Order & Chaos {#order-and-chaos}

**Essence.** Show that your work either tames a chaotic situation or shakes loose one that has become too rigid, casting yourself or your user as the hero who finds the right balance.

**When to reach for it.** Use when you need to clarify the mission of a project by naming the system being changed and the kind of change being introduced.

**Why it works.** Every meaningful story sits at the seam between the safe-but-stale known and the dangerous-but-fertile unknown. Naming both worlds and showing how they collide makes the stakes obvious: too much order and the system suffocates, too much chaos and it collapses. Putting a hero between them gives the audience someone to identify with and a clear question — restore stability or unleash potential? — that drives the rest of the narrative.

**How to apply.**
- Describe the known world your protagonist operates in — its strengths, its rules, and what's stifling about it.
- Describe the unknown world pressing against it — competitors, new technology, shifting behaviour, or events outside anyone's control.
- Show how the unknown disrupts the known — what sudden change, anomaly, or new information forces a response.
- Position the hero's choice — are they imposing structure on a chaotic situation, or breathing new life into a stagnant one?

## Rules, Cheats & Rebels {#rules-cheats-and-rebels}

**Essence.** Name a rule, then show someone breaking it — the audience cannot look away from a transgression and will follow you to find out whether the breaker is punished or vindicated.

**When to reach for it.** Use when you're telling a story about an innovator, a disruptor, or anyone whose work depends on defying conventional wisdom or breaking a category norm.

**Why it works.** Rule-breaking is one of the oldest narrative engines because we live inside countless rules — written and unwritten — and we pay close attention to anyone who flouts them. The interesting move is to separate two flavours of rule-breaker: the rebel acts selflessly, hides nothing, and breaks rules to serve a higher one; the cheat acts selfishly, hides their actions, and breaks rules everyone else still has to follow. The same act looks heroic or villainous depending on motive and concealment, and choosing the right framing controls how your audience feels.

**How to apply.**
- Identify whose rules are being broken — yours, your user's, your industry's, or society's broadly held assumptions.
- Make the rule explicit so the break is visible, especially if the rule is unwritten or normative.
- If your hero is a rebel, show that they break the rule openly, pay a personal cost, and serve a higher principle — and end by retiring the broken rule.
- If your character is a cheat, show that they break the rule selfishly, hide their actions, force others to bear the cost — and end by re-affirming the rule.
- Decide the moral up front so every detail of the telling pushes the audience to the verdict you want.

## Secrets & Puzzles {#secrets-and-puzzles}

**Essence.** Open with a hidden thing the audience wants revealed or a contradiction they want resolved, because an open information gap is one of the strongest attention hooks there is.

**When to reach for it.** Use for the opening line of a pitch, the subject line of an email, the headline of a blog, or any moment where you need to win attention quickly.

**Why it works.** We are wired to close information gaps. A secret tells the audience that you possess knowledge they don't have, and they will stay with you to acquire it. A puzzle tells them that something doesn't add up — an anomaly, an irony, an inconsistency — and they will stay with you to resolve it. Both work by promising new information, so they only land if you actually deliver. Use them as a hook and then pay them off, or you've written clickbait.

**How to apply.**
- To open with a secret, point at something most people don't know — what was hidden, why it stayed hidden, and what audiences will gain by learning it.
- To open with a puzzle, surface an oddity — a result that defies expectation, a behaviour that contradicts a stated value, an outcome that breaks a pattern — and promise to make sense of it.
- Be sure the payoff genuinely delivers new information, or the trick erodes trust for next time.
- Test the opening by checking it would survive being read aloud — does it create an itch the audience would actively scratch?

## Shock of the Old {#shock-of-the-old}

**Essence.** Mine objects and practices that have survived for generations for the deep insights they encode, because anything that has lasted that long is solving a problem we still have.

**When to reach for it.** Use when fascination with novelty is starving your story of substance, or when you need to make a point feel timeless by rooting it in something the audience already trusts.

**Why it works.** New things usually fail. Old things — objects, customs, professions — that are still around carry hidden wisdom about human needs, because if they didn't serve some deep purpose they'd have disappeared long ago. Studying an enduring object closely reveals layers of obvious and hidden function, of symbolism, and of what it says about us. Connecting your story to that durable wisdom gives it gravity and a sense of inevitability that a story about a shiny new thing rarely achieves.

**How to apply.**
- Pick an old object or practice your audience or user lives with and ask what story it's telling.
- Catalogue the obvious purposes — what it visibly does.
- Probe the hidden purposes — emotional, social, ritual, symbolic functions that aren't on the label.
- Note who owns it, who uses it, who adapts it, and what that ownership chain reveals.
- Surface the symbolic and ritual power it holds — what feelings it triggers and why.
- Connect those layers back to the point you're making so your story inherits the object's accumulated authority.

## The Dragon & the City {#the-dragon-and-the-city}

**Essence.** Recast your project as a city threatened by a dragon, with options of fleeing, fortifying, or attacking — a primal frame that makes ordinary business choices feel urgent and consequential.

**When to reach for it.** Use when you need to energise stakeholders around a strategic decision and want the choices to feel heroic and high-stakes rather than incremental.

**Why it works.** Abstract business questions are hard to feel. Translating them into a fairy-tale-grade threat to a fortified home makes the stakes concrete and the options vivid. The city stands for the status quo — comfortable, valuable, limiting. The dragon stands for the disruptive threat or opportunity bearing down on it. Each of the three responses — run, defend, attack — comes with costs and rewards, which forces an honest conversation about trade-offs that a flat strategy memo would never produce.

**How to apply.**
- Define the city — what is valuable and worth protecting in the current state, what is unfair or wasteful, and who holds the keys.
- Define the dragon — where the threat is coming from, why it grew, and what spoils it guards.
- Lay out the option to escape — where you would go, what you'd take, and the cost of abandoning what you have.
- Lay out the option to defend — what you'd reinforce, what new restrictions that creates, and what you'd give up to stay safe.
- Lay out the option to attack — your best line, your odds, and whether the reward justifies the risk.
- Make the trade-offs explicit so the audience feels the weight of choosing one path over the others.

## Three Great Conflicts {#three-great-conflicts}

**Essence.** Pick the right fight at the heart of your story — your hero against nature, against other people, or against themselves — because every memorable story turns on one of these three.

**When to reach for it.** Use when defining the central tension of a pitch, narrative, or campaign, especially when the work feels meandering or low-stakes.

**Why it works.** Audiences engage with stories in proportion to the conflict at their core. The three classic fights map to three different anxieties: survival against an indifferent world, struggle for resources or values against other people, and the internal battle between who we are and who we know we should be. Sharp stories pick one as primary. The trick is to first articulate the dream version of your world and the nightmare version, then locate the conflict at the seam between them and decide which great fight you're actually telling.

**How to apply.**
- Sketch the dream — what your project's world looks like when everything goes right.
- Sketch the nightmare — what it looks like when everything goes wrong.
- Locate the seams where dream meets nightmare; those are the live conflicts in your story.
- Choose the central fight — survival against the world, opposition with other people, or the internal struggle against one's own worst tendencies.
- Show how the conflict resolves, even imperfectly, so the audience leaves with a sense of where the energy went.

## Universal Stories {#universal-stories}

**Essence.** Build your story on the human traits everyone shares across cultures, because those universals are the most reliable foundation for an audience to recognise themselves in.

**When to reach for it.** Use when you're addressing an audience whose backgrounds differ widely and you need to find the common ground that lets every listener connect.

**Why it works.** Cultures vary enormously, but a handful of patterns show up everywhere: we feel like agents making real choices, we cooperate and conflict, our actions don't match our stated ideals, we know fear and courage, we have a shared sense of fairness and judgement, and we all pass through recognisable life stages. Stories anchored in these universals travel further and stick longer than stories that depend on local references, because the audience doesn't have to be taught what the building blocks mean.

**How to apply.**
- Ground a story in agency and choice when you want listeners to feel that real decisions are being made.
- Ground it in cooperation versus competition when the action turns on whether people work together or against one another.
- Ground it in the gap between what we say and what we do when the point is hypocrisy, blind spots, or self-deception.
- Ground it in fear and courage when overcoming danger is the heart of the action.
- Ground it in fairness and judgement when audiences need to feel the verdict is just.
- Ground it in life-stage transitions when you're describing growth, coming-of-age, or a graduation from one phase to another.
