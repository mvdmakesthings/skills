# Tuesday pitch: prep plan

Before I draft anything, here's how I'm reading your situation, because the diagnosis dictates the shape.

## Diagnosis

- **Audience:** VP of Marketing, has bought three analytics tools in five years, none got adopted. She is not skeptical of analytics. She is skeptical of *being sold to about analytics*. She has scar tissue.
- **Goal:** sell — move her toward a pilot or purchase decision in 30 minutes.
- **Asset you have:** three live pilots, two of them well-known brands. That is your most expensive and least-used weapon. Lead with it, but not the way most reps do.
- **The trap to avoid:** the standard "Stories that Sell" recipe leans on *Rags to Riches* — an aspirational ascent arc where your customer transforms. With a three-strikes buyer, that arc sounds exactly like what the last three vendors said. A clean optimistic story will get her nodding politely and saying "send me materials."

## The shape I'm going to apply

I'm taking the **Stories that Sell** recipe as the spine — Audience Profile, Simple Sales Stories, Social Proof, an aspirational arc, Pitch Perfect — but swapping the structural arc from *Rags to Riches* to **No Easy Way**, and opening with **Secrets & Puzzles** instead of a normal hook.

Why the swap:

- *No Easy Way* is the arc for audiences who would find a smooth story suspicious. Real adoption is bumpy. She knows it. Telling the bumpy version is the credibility move.
- *Secrets & Puzzles* opens with the puzzle she is already living — "you've bought three tools and none got used; here's what actually breaks adoption" — so you are speaking her thought out loud before she has to.
- *Pride & Fall* lives in the background as an acknowledged risk: you name out loud the failure mode of her last three purchases before she does.

Net tactic stack: **Secrets & Puzzles (opener) → Audience Profile (frame) → No Easy Way (arc, told through one pilot) → Social Proof (the other two) → Hero & Guide (positioning) → Pitch Perfect (the 60-second version she can repeat to her CEO).**

Sound right? If your read of her is different — say she's actually a true believer who just got unlucky on vendors, not scarred — tell me and I'll re-shape toward a cleaner *Rags to Riches*.

Below is the prep on the assumption I've called it correctly.

## The 30-minute structure

Spend ~7 minutes earning the right to pitch, ~15 on the story, ~5 on the ask, ~3 buffer. The hard rule: she should be talking inside the first three minutes.

### Minute 0–3 — Open with the puzzle, not the product

Do not open with a slide. Do not open with your company history. Open with one sentence that names her experience.

> "Most marketing leaders I talk to have bought two or three analytics tools that ended up unused. I want to spend a few minutes on what we've learned about *why* that happens before I show you anything we built — because if the reason it happened to you isn't a reason we've solved, this is the wrong meeting."

Then stop talking. Let her respond. She will, because you just named the thing she was bracing to defend against, and you handed her the option to end the meeting — which paradoxically makes her want to stay.

*(Tactic: Secrets & Puzzles — the puzzle is "why don't analytics tools get used?" She's been living inside that puzzle. You're offering to resolve it.)*

### Minute 3–7 — Audience Profile, said back to her

You should already know, before walking in, the answer to these three:

1. What metrics is her team currently reporting on, and to whom?
2. What is the one report her CEO or board asks for that her team struggles to produce?
3. What did her last three tools promise, and what specifically broke? (Adoption? Data integration? Insight quality? Workflow fit?)

If you can't answer these, spend an hour today getting answers — LinkedIn, mutual connections, her team's job postings, her conference talks. Walk in knowing.

In the meeting, *say her situation back to her* in two or three sentences. Not "tell me about your goals." That's the question of someone who didn't do the work.

> "From what I can see, your team is reporting weekly on campaign ROI and pipeline contribution, your CEO keeps asking for attribution clarity you can't quite give him, and the tools you tried before promised dashboards but never got past your analytics team into the marketers' daily workflow. Is that the right shape of it?"

She corrects you on one detail. That correction is gold. Use it for the next 20 minutes.

*(Tactic: Audience Profile — but performed, not gathered. You're proving you did the homework.)*

### Minute 7–22 — The arc, told through one pilot

This is the body. Pick **the messier of your two well-known-brand pilots** — not the cleaner one. Counterintuitive, but right: messy stories are credible.

Tell it as a *No Easy Way* arc, in five beats. Aim for 12–15 minutes including her questions.

**Beat 1 — Bad place (1 min).** Where the customer started. Their version of her problem. Be specific: "Their CMO was getting attribution numbers that didn't match their CFO's revenue numbers by 18%. Every QBR turned into a fight about whose data was right."

**Beat 2 — Early opportunity (2 min).** What gave them their first real signal that something could change. Not your product yet — the *moment*. "Three weeks in, their demand gen lead pulled one report that finally tied a campaign to a closed-won deal at the rep level. First time in two years."

**Beat 3 — The setback (3 min).** *This is the beat most reps skip and it's the most important one.* Tell her what went wrong. Adoption stalled. The data team pushed back. A senior marketer hated the UI. *Something*. If your pilots are perfect, you don't have pilots, you have demos.

> "About six weeks in, adoption flattened. Two of their senior marketers stopped logging in. We dug in and found they didn't trust the numbers because we hadn't reconciled to their Salesforce source-of-truth report. We had to spend two weeks on data lineage work that wasn't in the original scope."

**Beat 4 — The recovery (4 min).** What you and they learned in the dark. The specific change — process, product, integration, training — that turned it around. The lesson that now shapes how you onboard everyone.

**Beat 5 — Better place (2 min).** Where they are now. One concrete result, ideally a behavior change not a number. "Their Monday marketing standup now opens with a live attribution view instead of a slide deck someone built Friday afternoon. The CMO's QBR fights are over."

Then pause and say: "I told you the rough version on purpose. The smooth version is what your last three vendors sold you."

She will exhale. That's the moment the meeting turns.

*(Tactics: No Easy Way structure; Hero & Guide positioning — the customer is the hero of the story, you and your team are the guide who helped them through the dark middle.)*

### Minute 22–25 — The other two pilots, fast

Now and only now do you do social proof. Two sentences each. No logos parade.

> "Brand B is six months in — their CMO is now using us as the source for her board deck. Brand C is three months in — we're still in the rough middle I just described, and I can tell you which week we expect it to turn."

Naming that Brand C is still in the messy middle is a power move. It says: I'm not hiding anything from you, and I won't hide anything from you if you become customer four.

*(Tactic: Social Proof — but inoculated against skepticism by admitting where it's incomplete.)*

### Minute 25–28 — The ask, and the 60-second version

The ask is **not** "do you want to buy this." The ask is the next reversible step.

> "Here's what I'd like. A two-week structured pilot with one of your campaigns — your choice which one — where success is defined by your team, not mine, before we start. If at the end of two weeks your senior marketers aren't logging in voluntarily, we walk away and you owe nothing. The reason I'm pitching this and not a 60-day pilot is because the failure mode of every tool you've bought has been adoption, and I want adoption to be the gate, not features."

Then give her the **Pitch Perfect** version — the 60-second story she can repeat to her CEO or CFO when they ask "who was that meeting with":

> "I met with [your company]. They sold one of our peers an analytics platform with an adoption guarantee — two weeks, our team decides if it gets used, otherwise no charge. Two of their three pilot customers are using it as their primary attribution source. I want to run the two-week pilot on the Q3 brand campaign."

That sentence is what actually gets her to a yes. She is not going to remember your story. She is going to remember the version she has to tell to someone else. Write that sentence *for* her.

*(Tactic: Pitch Perfect — the verbal-tweet version she can carry out of the room.)*

### Minute 28–30 — Buffer and silence

Do not fill these. If she has questions, let them come. If she doesn't, end early. Ending five minutes early on a Tuesday pitch makes you the only vendor who didn't overrun.

## Three things to bring, three things to leave behind

**Bring:**

1. A printed one-pager of the pilot terms — adoption-gated, two weeks, defined-by-her-team success. Hand it to her at minute 25. The physical artifact makes it real.
2. The name and email of the demand gen lead from your messy pilot, with permission to be called. "Don't take my word for the rough middle. Call her."
3. One screen — *one* — of the dashboard the messy-pilot CMO actually looks at on Monday. Not a product tour. One screen, with one sentence about why it changed her Monday.

**Leave behind:**

1. The standard deck. If you have a 30-slide pitch deck, do not bring it. It will pull you back into vendor-mode.
2. Feature lists. She has seen four of them in five years. They are not what's missing.
3. ROI calculators. Skeptics don't move on math; they move on trust. Math comes after she says yes to the pilot.

## The one question you need to answer before Tuesday

I can scaffold all of this, but the story has one slot only you can fill: **the specific moment of the setback** in the messy pilot. The Beat 3 detail.

Tell me, in one or two sentences:

- What is the concrete thing that went wrong in your messiest pilot's first 90 days?
- What did you and the customer specifically do to fix it?

If you can answer those, I can sharpen Beats 3 and 4 into the version you actually deliver on Tuesday. That is the beat the whole pitch lives or dies on.
