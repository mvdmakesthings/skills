# Pitch Prep: B2B Analytics to a Burned VP of Marketing

Great context to work with. The single most important thing here: **she is not skeptical of analytics — she is skeptical of adoption.** That reframes the entire pitch. If you walk in talking about features, dashboards, or "AI-powered insights," you've lost before minute five. She's heard it three times.

Here's how I'd prep.

## The core insight to anchor everything on

Her real question isn't "is your product good?" It's "why won't this end up like the other three?"

Every slide, every demo moment, every answer to a question should ladder up to answering *that*. If you internalize this, your prep gets a lot easier because most decisions become obvious.

## Suggested 30-minute structure

I'd split it roughly like this:

- **0–3 min — Acknowledge the elephant.** Open with something like: "Before I show you anything, I want to name something. You've probably bought analytics tools before that nobody used. That's the problem we obsess over — not dashboards, adoption." This disarms her. She came in ready to deflect a sales pitch; you just told her you know why the last three failed.
- **3–8 min — The "why tools die" diagnosis.** Briefly walk through the 2–3 reasons B2B analytics tools fail to get used (too much setup, insights aren't trusted, no one's job depends on opening it, etc.). She'll be nodding. You're now on her side of the table.
- **8–18 min — Pilot proof, told as stories.** Don't show a feature matrix. Tell the story of one of your two well-known brand pilots: what their world looked like before, what changed in week 1, week 4, and what their team actually does with it now. Quantify *usage*, not just outcomes — "47 of 60 marketers log in weekly" hits harder than "23% lift in campaign ROI" with this audience.
- **18–24 min — Live demo, but narrow.** Show one workflow end-to-end — ideally the one most relevant to her org. Resist showing breadth. Breadth is what the last three vendors did.
- **24–30 min — The adoption plan, not the contract.** Close with how you'd specifically drive adoption in her org: who you'd onboard first, what the 30/60/90 looks like, what success criteria you'd commit to in writing. This is your differentiator.

## Things to prepare in advance

1. **Get the names of the three tools she bought.** If you can find this out before Tuesday (LinkedIn, mutual contacts, even just asking her assistant), you can subtly reference the failure patterns of those specific categories. If you can't, ask her in the first 5 minutes: "Out of curiosity, which tools did you try?" Her answer tells you which objections to preempt.
2. **One killer pilot quote.** Get a direct quote from a champion at one of your well-known pilots — ideally something like "we actually use this" or "my team asks for it now." Put it on one slide. That's your trust anchor.
3. **A written 90-day adoption commitment.** Bring a one-pager: "Here's what we'd commit to in your first 90 days. If we don't hit X usage metric, here's what happens." Money-back, free extension, whatever's appropriate. The willingness to put adoption in writing is the single biggest signal that you're different.
4. **Three references ready.** Have the pilot customers prepped to take a call within 48 hours of the meeting. Mention this casually: "I can have you on the phone with [Brand X]'s VP this week."

## Objections to rehearse

Have crisp 60-second answers for:

- "How is this different from [Tableau / Looker / Amplitude / whatever she'll name]?" — Answer in terms of adoption mechanics, not features.
- "How long until my team is actually using it?" — Have a specific number from your pilots. Not a hope, a number.
- "What happens if my team doesn't adopt it?" — This is where the written commitment comes out.
- "Why should I believe the pilots will translate to my org?" — Talk about what the pilots had in common with her situation (size, sector, marketing org structure).

## Tonal notes

- **Don't oversell.** She's allergic to it. Be the vendor who undersells slightly and overdelivers in the room.
- **Ask more than you tell in the first 10 minutes.** "What did the last three tools get wrong for your team?" is a great question. Let her diagnose; you prescribe.
- **Bring one printed page, not a deck.** Or bring a deck but be ready to abandon it. VPs who've been burned want a conversation, not a presentation.
- **Don't talk about your roadmap.** She doesn't care what's coming. She cares what works on Tuesday.

## What to leave behind

A one-pager with: the pilot results (usage + outcome), the 90-day adoption commitment, two reference contacts, and your direct line. Nothing else. She'll forward it internally — make sure it survives the forward.

## The one-line version of your pitch

If you only get one sentence in the elevator after: *"We're the analytics product that VPs of Marketing actually see their teams using six months in — and we'll put that in writing."*
