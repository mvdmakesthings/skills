# Q4 Miss Memo — Draft

Before I draft, here's the shape I'm using and why, so you can redirect if it's wrong:

- **Structure: *Man in a Hole*.** Settled state → fall → what we learned in the dark → deliberate climb. This arc lets you hold *both* causes (external softening and internal pricing) inside one honest narrative, and it ends in a place that's wiser than where Q4 started. Execs respect this shape because it tracks how real businesses actually move.
- **Failure frame: *Epic Fail* as "system breakdown" + "sign of the times."** These are the two honest frames that don't require a scapegoat. The pricing change is owned at the leadership level as a process call that didn't hold up — not a sales execution problem. The November softening is named for what it was: weather, not character. Choosing the frame deliberately is what keeps the memo from sliding into either denial or self-flagellation.
- **Character: *Hero & Guide*.** The exec team and the sales org are the heroes here — they ran the play they were given. You are the guide. The miss is yours; the climb is theirs to lead with your support. This is the move that keeps sales out from under the bus without sounding like you're protecting them, which would be worse.
- **Style: *Leave it Out!* + *Three is the Magic Number*.** Memo discipline — lead with the news, no throat-clearing, three recovery actions (not seven), and stop. Execs will scroll if you give them anything to scroll past.

Sound right? If yes, here's the draft.

---

## Memo: Q4 finished 18% below plan. Here's what happened and what we're doing.

**To:** Executive team
**From:** [you]
**Re:** Q4 result and Q1 path

Q4 came in 18% below the number. I want to walk you through what I think happened, what I'm taking responsibility for, and the three things we're changing in Q1.

### Where we were going in

We entered Q4 with a healthy pipeline and a sales org executing well against the prior plan. Forecast confidence was reasonable. Nothing in the early-quarter signal said "miss."

### What knocked us down

Two things hit us, and I want to name them separately because the response to each is different.

The first was external. The market softened through November in a way that showed up across our segment, not just in our numbers. Deals that would have closed in a normal quarter stretched into December or slipped to Q1. That's the part we didn't cause and couldn't have prevented — but we should have read it sooner.

The second was internal, and it's mine. The pricing change we rolled out early in the quarter compressed close rates more than our modeling predicted. The structure was sound on paper; the field reality of it under a softening market was not something our process was set up to catch in time. That's a system call I made, and I want to be direct: this was not a sales execution problem. The team ran the play they were given. I gave them a play that didn't hold up against the conditions, and I didn't have the early-warning instrumentation in place to course-correct before it cost us the quarter.

The 18% sits at the intersection of those two forces. Either one alone would have been a tougher quarter. Together they were the miss.

### What we learned in the hole

Three things are now clear that weren't clear in September:

1. Our pricing-change rollout process needs a fast-read feedback loop on close-rate movement, not a quarterly retrospective.
2. Our forecasting model under-weights macro signal from our segment. We were reading our own pipeline and not the market our pipeline sits inside.
3. The cost of a pricing experiment isn't just the deals it directly affects — it's the confidence drag on every adjacent deal in flight. We need to treat pricing changes as the higher-risk events they are.

### The climb

Three actions in Q1, owned at the exec level:

1. **Roll back the specific pricing element that compressed close rates**, effective immediately, while we redesign it with field input. We are not going to debate this for a month.
2. **Stand up a weekly close-rate and market-condition read** that surfaces movement inside three weeks, not three months. I'll own the design; [name] will own the operating cadence.
3. **Re-baseline the Q1 forecast against the slower November/December close behavior** so we're planning against the world that exists, not the one we wanted.

I'm not promising Q1 makes back what Q4 lost. I am telling you we now have a clearer read on what bent, why it bent, and what we're doing differently. The team that ran Q4 is the same team I want running Q1 — they earned that, and the change they need is upstream of them, not from them.

Happy to walk any of this through in person. I'd rather have the harder conversation now than the easier one later.

## Notes on the draft

Tactics used: *Man in a Hole* (arc), *Epic Fail / system-breakdown + sign-of-the-times* (failure frame), *Hero & Guide* (you are the guide; sales and execs are the heroes), *Three is the Magic Number* (three lessons, three actions), *Leave it Out!* (memo discipline).

What I deliberately left out, so you can decide whether to put it back:

- **Numbers inside the memo.** I kept it to the 18% and avoided breakdowns by region, segment, or rep. If your exec team will want a "show your work" appendix, add it below the signature, not above — it changes the document's center of gravity.
- **Naming the pricing change specifically.** I left it abstract so the memo doesn't become a referendum on that one decision. If your execs already know which change you mean, naming it explicitly will read as more honest, not less.
- **A timeline for the Q1 actions.** I gave you "immediately," "weekly," and "re-baseline" — but no dates. Add a date next to each action if your culture treats undated commitments as soft.

## Two things only you can fill in

1. **The forecast-vs-reality moment.** Was there a specific week or signal in November where, in hindsight, you could have called the miss and didn't? Naming that moment — even briefly — would make the "we should have read it sooner" line land much harder than it does now.
2. **The team's morale read.** I wrote this assuming sales is bruised but intact. If they're actually shaken, the memo needs one more sentence — probably near the end — that signals to the exec team how you want them to show up for the sales org in the next two weeks.
